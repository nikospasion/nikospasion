---
title: You are what you consume
date: 2026-06-23
---

## No true originality

I think that there is no true originality. Everything is subjective at some point. We see things, internalize them, feed them into our mind, and that thing, whatever it is, gets modified or manipulated by our own lived experiences. It gets filtered into our own lenses and we make it a version of our own. And I think creativity sits with what we do with what we’ve consumed. We take influences, experiences, half-formed feelings, things we’ve read, and arrange them in a way that reflects your particular inner configuration. Nobody else has the exact configuration, so nobody else would have made the exact thing.

But if everything is filtered, does that mean nothing is shared? I think no. While it’s true that every perception, every interpretation is already colored by who you are when you encountered it, they are built from the same kinds of raw material. Grief. Desire. Every thing that is human. Subjective. And subjectivity is usually said like it’s a problem. Like objectivity is the goal. And yet, these are specific to each person in their details but recognizable across all persons in their shape. And because these live within ourselves, and the filter is the self, subjectivity gives the “thing” a meaning. Strip that away — no person, no filter, no self — you get nothing. Subjectivity is the reason why art travels, not because it’s objective.

## Where the creative act lives

That’s why the more you consume, the more raw material the filter has to work with — which is a quietly radical idea! It relocates the creative act:

```
    a book             a film            a conversation
   you read          you watched         that moved you            CREATING
      |                  |                    |                       |
      o - - - - - - - -  o - - - - - - - - -  o             [------- (•) -------]   TIME
      |_____________________________________________________________|
                        WHERE IT ACTUALLY IS

 (where we think it is: just the small bracketed section around CREATING)
```

We tend to fetishize the output because it’s more rewarding than the input. We only celebrate the painting, the song, the idea. But the real creative act might be the quality of attention you brought to everything before you made the thing. What you noticed, what moved you, what you let in.

## Create, don’t consume

It can be ironic sometimes when people say: “Create, don’t consume.” But consumption was never the problem — it was unhealthy, unconscious consumption. I do believe that every artist, every creator we admire was an obsessive consumer first. They allowed themselves to feel and absorb things. The consuming was the becoming.

## Making it yours

So how do we create something that is ours? Maybe the answer is: trusting the output of your own processing before anyone else has had the chance to touch it.

```
   ( WHAT YOU CONSUMED )      ( WHAT YOU EXPERIENCED )
              \                        /
               \                      /
          ( WHAT YOU EXPRESSED HONESTLY )

   · NOT LETTING ANY EXTERNAL INPUTS INTERFERE
   · NOT MOLDING IT INTO SOMETHING “ACCEPTABLE”
```

I think, genuinely, that something can feel yours when you stopped trying to make it look legitimate, and just let it be what your particular life turned it into.

There’s also something about not pushing the “digestion.” Sometimes, not all the time, things do need time to become yours. If you consume something and immediately reproduce it, it still has the original’s fingerprints all over it. But if you let it sit, let it collide with your experiences, let it get strange, it stops being theirs and starts being yours.

```
YOU ARE WHAT YOU CONSUME.
YOUR WORK IS PROOF OF WHAT YOU ATE.
```
